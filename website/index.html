REALISM

Focuses on depicting subjects as they appear in real life, with attention to detail and accuracy.

"Impression, Sunrise" by Claude Monet.

IMPRESSIONISM

Captures the effects of light and color with loose brushwork, often painted outdoors.

"The Scream" by Edvard Munch.

EXPRESSIONISM

Emphasizes emotional experience over physical reality, often with bold colors and dynamic brushstrokes.

"Man with a Guitar" by Georges Braque

CUBISM

The artwork deconstructs the subject into a series of geometric shapes and overlapping planes. It emphasizes form over color, using a limited palette of browns, grays, and blacks.

"The Persistence of Memory" by Salvador Dalí

SURREALISM

Known for its melting clocks, this surrealist painting explores the fluidity of time and the impermanence of reality. It’s a visual representation of Dalí's fascination with dreams and the subconscious mind.

"Woman I" by Willem de Kooning

ABSTRACT EXPRESSIONISM

The painting can be seen as a commentary on the complex and often contradictory nature of femininity and beauty. It also represents the raw emotion and spontaneity central to Abstract Expressionism.

Works by Banksy

STREET ART

Created in public spaces, often with a focus on social or political commentary, using various techniques like graffiti.

Union I by Frank Stella

MINIMALISM

Focusing only on boiling down the painting only to the subject’s essential elements and avoiding excessive detail, minimalism simplifies a subject’s form through minimal use of geometric shapes, monochromatic color harmonies, and clean lines.

John's Diner with John's Chevelle, by John Baeder

PHOTOREALISM

Photorealistic paintings often try to recreate objects from taken photographs, often to an extreme degree. There is an extreme level of detail present, and artists often use photographs themselves as reference images
